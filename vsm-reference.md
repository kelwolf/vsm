# Viable System Model — Reference

*Mandated structural foundation for organizational architecture. Derived from Stafford Beer (1972), the VSM Guide (Frahm, SCiO), and primary cybernetics literature.*

---

## Foundation

The VSM is built on Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety: a system can only survive if its internal variety matches or exceeds the variety of its environment. Only variety can absorb variety.

The Conant-Ashby theorem (Good Regulator): every effective regulator of a system must be a model of that system. The quality of organizational management depends on the fidelity of its underlying model.

POSIWID: The purpose of the system is what it does.

---

## Five Subsystems

Every viable system requires five subsystems. These are necessary and sufficient for viability.

### System 1 — Operation
The primary activities. What the organization does. Each System 1 unit has three elements: its environment, its operation, and its management. An organization may have multiple System 1 units.

### System 2 — Coordination
Coordinates between System 1 units. Provides standardized processes, common language, self-organization space. Prevents oscillation and conflict between operational units. Examples: scheduling, shared standards, coordination protocols.

A well-tuned System 2: 70% self-coordination, 20% active coordination, 10% individual decisions from System 3.

### System 3 — Operational Management (Inside and Now)
Manages the internal environment. Allocates resources, sets performance expectations, optimizes current operations. Ensures synergy between System 1 units.

### System 3* — Audit/Monitoring
A review channel. Verifies that what System 1 reports matches reality. Ensures congruence between reported results and actual results. Examples: direct observation, audits, management walks.

### System 4 — Strategic Management (Outside and Then)
Faces the external environment. Handles future challenges, strategic planning, environmental scanning, innovation, development of new capabilities. Competes with System 3 for resources.

### System 5 — Policy/Identity
The identity of the organization. Values, norms, ethics, culture, purpose. The highest decision-making authority. Regulates the balance between System 3 (inside/now) and System 4 (outside/then). Without System 5, fragmentation is inevitable.

---

## Six Information Channels

### Channel 1 — Intervention and Regulation
Central specifications from System 5 to System 1. Rules, manuals, contracts. What is expected?

### Channel 2 — Resource Allocation
Resource distribution. Personnel, materials, budget. What is needed?

### Channel 3 — Operational Interrelationships
Informal inner relations. Culture, rituals, how people actually work together.

### Channel 4 — Environmental Interrelationships
How external and internal environments affect each other. Stakeholders, market, politics.

### Channel 5 — Coordination (System 2)
How coordination and control take place between System 1 units.

### Channel 6 — Monitoring/Audit (System 3*)
What is actually happening in production? Verifies congruence with reports.

## Recursion

The central structural principle: every viable system contains viable systems and is contained in a viable system. The organizational structure must be recursive — the same five-subsystem pattern applies at every level.

This means: if the unit is viable, scaling is instantiation, not redesign. A new engagement, a new team, a new division — each is a viable system with its own S1-S5, contained within the larger viable system.

---

## Five Variety Balances

### 1. Workload (S1 ↔ Environment)
Can the operational unit manage the variety from its environment? Can it sustain itself? Can it self-renew?

### 2. Line Balancing (S1 ↔ S1 via S2)
Are operational units coordinated? Are resources properly allocated between them? Is there adequate standardization?

### 3. Autonomy vs. Cohesion (S1 ↔ S3)
Is there sufficient autonomy for operations to execute? Is there sufficient cohesion for strategic action as a whole? Are vertical and horizontal axes in balance?

### 4. Change Rate (S4 ↔ Environment)
Is the organization adapting to changes in market and technology? Is it introducing capabilities without demand? Is it relying on obsolete capabilities?

### 5. Change vs. Status Quo (S3 ↔ S4 via S5)
Is there a balanced decision-making process between current operations and future development? Are resources being reallocated from existing to new activities when needed? Is identity maintained through change?

## Pathological Archetypes

### Fantasy World
Decisions made without sound information. Neither operational data nor environmental data informs leadership. Perception diverges from reality.

### Control Dilemma
Senior management fears loss of control, demands more reporting, issues more instructions. Operations face pressure from both environment and management. Management neglects strategic activities.

### Bottlenecks
Uncoordinated workload fluctuations between operational units and environment. Triggers control dilemma.

### Reinventing the Wheel
Insufficient standards. Multiple units independently solve the same problems. Resources wasted on duplicate work.

### Bunker Mentality
Organization closes off from environment during crisis. Information neither exchanged nor accepted. Isolation provides illusion of control.

## System Laws

**Ashby's Law:** Only variety can absorb variety.

**Recursive systems theorem:** If a viable system contains a viable system, the organizational structure must be recursive.

**Relaxation time principle:** System stability requires that recovery time is shorter than the interval between disturbances.

**Black box principle:** It is not necessary to understand the inner workings of a system to deal with the variety it produces. Focus on input-output relationships.

**Darkness principle:** No system can be completely captured. Systems change while being observed. Residual uncertainty must be accepted.

**Agility theorem:** The rate of organizational change must be greater than or equal to the rate of environmental change. ΔO ≥ ΔE.

**Order osmosis:** Elements flow from less organized systems to more organized systems. Competent people leave disordered environments for ordered ones.

**Homeostasis principle:** A system is stable if all key variables remain within physiological limits.

## Application to Organizational Architecture

The VSM satisfies the three foundational imperatives:

**Self-describing (function, structure, purpose visible):** S1-S3 make function visible. The five-subsystem structure makes structure visible. S5 makes purpose visible. All three are required, not optional.

**Self-connecting (division and reintegration):** S2 coordinates between operational units. S3 manages the internal whole. Channels 1-6 define every interface. No boundary exists without a defined connection across it.

**Self-varying (variety matches environment):** Built directly on Ashby's Law. S4 scans the environment. S5 balances current and future. Variety balances 1-5 provide diagnostic criteria at every level.

Recursive application ensures unit-level viability at every scale.
